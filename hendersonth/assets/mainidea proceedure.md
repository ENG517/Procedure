# Example Text
In 1848, the United States acquired California from Mexico, expanding American influence in the Pacific. On the other side of the ocean, America's expansion would soon change Japan as well. At this time Japan was still a medieval society where samurai warriors still lived in ancient castles and served powerful lords in a feudal system unchanged for centuries. The arrival of Commodore Matthew Perry's "Black Ships" in 1853 marked a pivotal moment in Japanese history. To the Japanese people, who had never seen steam-powered vessels or modern artillery, these ships seemed to come from another world. Perry's mission aimed to force Japan to open its ports for trade, Perry threatened to attack Japan's cities with his ships if they refused. The Japanese leadership, already alarmed by Britain's recent victory over China, recognized their vulnerability to Western military power. The agreements Japan signed with the U.S and Britian favored foreigners and soon the economy declined. This external pressure triggered dramatic change. In 1868, the Boshin War erupted, ending with the restoration of Emperor Meiji to power. The young emperor, though previously a ceremonial figure, became the symbol of a new Japan that would rapidly modernize to compete with Europe and America.

The Meiji government implemented sweeping reforms across society. They abolished the traditional class system, including the privileged status of the samurai warriors, many of whom struggled to find their place in a world where their medieval military skills had become obsolete. The government sent scholars abroad to study Western systems, established a modern military, built railroads, and created a public education system. Japan became the first Asian nation to successfully adopt Western-style industrialization. However, this rapid modernization created intense debates. Some advocated for complete Westernization, including democratic principles, while others argued for preserving traditional Japanese values while selectively adopting Western technology. Following the European imperial model, Japan built its own empire, defeating China (1895) and annexing Korea in 1910. Japan also became the first Asian country to defeat a European country in modern times, when they destroyed Russia's fleet in Asia (1905). Defeating Russia earned Japan global attention, and African American writers celeberated Japan's victory.

The question of democracy remained contentious. While Japan established a constitution and parliament in 1889, real power remained concentrated in a small elite. Relations with the United States became increasingly strained, especially after the Immigration Act of 1924 banned Japanese immigration to America. This discriminatory policy deeply hurt Japanese pride and contributed to growing anti-Western sentiment. Despite having been allies with Britain and the United States, Japan felt discriminated against by Western powers. This sense of rejection pushed Japan closer to Germany, another nation that felt constrained by the international order. This alignment, combined with Japan's imperial ambitions and internal political struggles, would eventually set the stage for World War II. The legacy of the Meiji period – rapid industrialization, military modernization, and the unresolved tension between democracy and dictatorship – would profoundly influence Japan's path in the decades to come.

## STAGING:
Looking at this text, we can see it's divided into three main paragraphs. Before diving in, notice that each paragraph seems to cover a different time period in Japanese history, but all relate to changes in Japan.
## COACHING:
### Finding clues 
Reading through, we spot several repeating themes: modernization, Western influence, and Japan's transformation. For instance there are the following cues

<li>The opening introduces U.S. influence in 1848<li>

<li>The mention of technology specifically 'western technology

</ul>

### Where is the main idea?
Putting all of these together, the main idea is: Japan's rapid transformation from a feudal society to a modern power during the Meiji period was driven by Western pressure and influence, leading to both modernization successes and new problems.
## ALERTING:
While there are fascinating details like Perry's "Black Ships" and the defeat of Russia's fleet, these are supporting details, not the main idea. Don't get caught up in specific dates or events - they support the larger story of Japan's transformation.
