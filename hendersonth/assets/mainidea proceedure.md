# Finding the Main Idea Procedure

## Introduction
Having trobule with finding the main idea in complex reading passages? We are here to help, linked is the example text we will be working with. We are using an example text about [World History](exampletext.md) proceeding to the steps below, please scan the passage, you do not have to read in detail. Followed by the instructions are a reference guide which break down what a main idea is, and ways to find it.

## Starting to look for main idea:
1. Looking at this text, we can see it's divided into three main paragraphs. Before diving in, notice that each paragraph seems to cover a different time period in Japanese history, but all relate to changes in Japan.

## Assess the main idea by following the events of the topic from the start to the end 
2. Reading through, we spot several repeating themes: modernization, Western influence, and Japan's transformation. For instance there are the following cues 
<ul>
<li>At the start of the passage Japan is a closed country<li>
<li>The opening introduces U.S. influence in 1848<li> 
<li>A struggle between different groups in Japan for power in a conflict called 'Boshin War<li>
<li>The mention of technology specifically 'western technology
<li>At the end of the text, Japan is colonizing other countries<li>
</ul>


## Don't get distracted by overly specific details!:
3. While there are fascinating details like Perry's "Black Ships" and the defeat of Russia's fleet, these are supporting details, not the main idea. Don't get caught up in specific dates or events - they support the larger story of Japan's transformation. Sometimes, reading passages will overfill a passage with details, don't let the overinformation distract you from your goal of finding out what the passage about and then answering the questions. 

## How do we use the supporting details to find the main idea?
4. Putting all of these together, the main idea is: Japan's rapid transformation from a feudal society to a modern power during the Meiji period was driven by Western pressure and influence, leading to both modernization successes and new problems.
