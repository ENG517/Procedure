Addressing a Read to Learn to Do Audience and Writing to Guide Actions
The audience described by Redish as "read to learn to do" was the focus of building the Diffit guide. Material on the pages is developed in such a way that it serves the educator in both understanding what Diffit can offer and how its features should be translated into effective lesson planning. Having followed Hart-Davidson's approach of "writing to guide actions, " each section has well-articulated steps a user can follow with much ease. Such a section would include "Adding Vocabulary Words," which is in detail ascribable in terms of what the users are to do in order to make sure that almost immediate application of what they have been shown has taken place.


Organization into Tasks
This tutorial is organized around distinct tasks as suggested by Carey et al. in Chapter 3. That is, for every major feature of Diffit-for instance, "Adding Vocabulary Words", "Adapted Reading Passage", "Adding Questions"-a different task is made with its own steps. This makes it easier for users to locate and follow through on specific actions to be performed in the Diffit platform.


Following Conventional Writing Styles
The conventional writing styles Hart-Davidson and van der Meij and Gellevij also suggested were taken into consideration in creating this guide. This guide makes use of straightforward and clear language and tries not to use jargon in any sense. Material is presented in logical order, going from the general-introductory material to the specifics of a step-by-step walk through the different features of Diffit. There are lists enumerated wherever such would apply in specifying steps for a process, and which would make said process easy to follow. Headings and subheadings have been used throughout the guide to organize the information presented and to facilitate scanning.

 

 


Applying Basic Docs-as-Code Editorial Workflow
Even though this is not visible in this output, the tutorial was produced in a docs-as-code way. It is written in Markdown syntax, which is very easily version-controlled and collaboratively edited using a Git-based system. Of course, this makes the maintenance of this documentation easy, integrated with the development process of the software.


Using Consistent Markdown Syntax
This tutorial consistently uses Markdown syntax throughout:. Headings are set with '#' symbols, lists are made either by numerical or bullet-point enumerations, and emphases are enclosed between asterisks or underscores. Syntax applied correctly. This consistent use of Markdown will ensure that the document renders appropriately across different platforms and can easily be converted to other formats if that is a requirement.
