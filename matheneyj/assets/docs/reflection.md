## How do your procedures address the needs of a "reading to learn to do audience"? Provide some specific examples that connect back to Redish's noted features of such an audience.
My procedures address the needs of a "reading to learn to do" audience by focusing on the user's goal and the task that needs to be accomplished, but still providing clarifying details and contextual information that will help the user actually learn about the topic rather than simply completing an action. For example, I include an optional, "letting go" and allowing the user to decide for themselves, based on the knowledge they've received so far, what the best course of action is for their context. I also provide details on risk and success in the alerting procedures, cautioning against proceeding without cleaning the damaged area, for example, as it will further damage the car. In one of the coaching steps, I also explain what the tools actually do, describing how the scratch remover liquid acts as an abrasive to smooth out the rough edges of a scratch or scrape. Later on, I caution that the scratch remover can damage the paint, further revealing the tool's properties and providing the reader with greater knowledge.


## How do your procedures follow the WTGA Staging, Coaching, and Alerting stylistic conventions? Provide some specific examples that connect back to the criteria from Hart-Davidson.
My procedures follow the WTGA stylistic conventions from Hart-Davidson by clearly stating the task to be completed and what the outcome will be.

> With a few tools and a little time, you can remove a minor scratch at home.

This phrase clearly indicates what the user will need and what they will be doing the accomplish their task. My alerting steps are also visually distinctive, labelled with **bold** text and the word "caution." Following the textbook reading, the altering steps begin with what to/not to do, followed by the risk warning, in order for users to reach the "see-think-use" process. My coaching steps all begin with an imperative verb phrase and detail what action should be taken and what the outcome will be.


## How do your procedures follow your task orientation work? In other words, based on your audience and their goals, discuss how you oriented your SCA moves to the tasks.
My procedures follow the task orientation work with the inclusion or pictures, which are helpful to orient a beginner user. My user scenario describes someone familiar with basic repair but not cosmetic damage or autobody repair, which qualifies as a beginner in dealing with exterior damage. The staging clearly describes what damage the steps address: shallow-medium deep scratches that don't reveal the metal underneath the paint. The simple and short coaching steps make the task digestible and clear for my audience with little-to-no expertise, but would be unsuitable for an intermediate-expert level audience.
