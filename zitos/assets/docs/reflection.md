# Procedure Assignment Reflection

## How do your procedures address the needs of a "reading to learn to do audience"? Provide some specific examples that connect back to Redish's noted features of such an audience.

I provided my audience with a step-by-step manual with both instructions and pictures to help orient them to the new task. The procedures are relatively simple and short making it easier for the audience to read and understand the tasks. Readers should be able to perform the basic tasks after following the instructions. I have achieved this by keeping the writing brief and giving realistic scenarios. I also provided alerts to the users so they would not skip/ignore especially important steps. Some steps also have context provided so the users understand why the step is important. For example, I explained the importance of cleaning the lint trap in order to prevent fires.

## How do your procedures follow the WTGA Staging, Coaching, and Alerting stylistic conventions? Provide some specific examples that connect back to the criteria from Hart-Davidson.

I started each procedure with a quick introduction to the article that orients readers. This staging includes a description of the task and what the reader will complete after following these instructions. Additionally, I listed the required materials that a user would need to complete the task. For the coaching stage, I broke the instructions down into small, numbered steps. Each step starts with an imperative phrase to make the instructions clear and simple to read. I also incorporated visual aids in my procedures to show users where certain buttons or compartments were located. Finally, I added some alerts throughout the procedures when they were necessary and bolded the font so they stood out.


## How do your procedures follow your task orientation work? In other words, based on your audience and their goals, discuss how you oriented your SCA moves to the tasks.

One of my users has experience washing clothes but is new to the NC State machines, the other has never washed clothes and is living in an apartment. First, I made the steps of my second procedure more concrete. For example, I tell the user which suggested settings to use. Experienced users do not need this information as they know which settings they like to use. I also provided more details for my beginner use, for example, where certain components were located like the lint trap. Another aspect was the type of machines being used. Although both users are NC State students, they live in different buildings, therefore the laundry machines are different. I changed my visual aids and some instructions depending on what laundry facilities the student would be using.

## How did you apply a basic docs-as-code editorial workflow to your assignment? Please provide specific cases with screenshots and/or links that can support your claims.

I used a docs-as-code editorial workflow throughout my assignment. To start, I used separate branches for each of my procedures to separate the content and to avoid conflict with the main branch. I wrote my procedures using markdown. Utilizing markdown, I added headings, text, and images (and used internal links to display these images). I pushed commits to save my work after working on different sections or files in my procedure. Once I was ready, I drafted my pull requests detailing my changes, users, and questions I wanted my reviewers to answer. I pushed this request to GitHub and assigned the pull request to my groupmates. Once they reviewed my work and left comments, I made edits to my documentation and pushed my final commits before merging my branches with the main branch.
